# 02 — Reading of the landscape

## Adversarial-AI / multi-agent-critique frontier (2024–2026)

The field has converged in an awkward way: the *prompting / multi-agent / debate* literature and the *inference-time-compute / reasoning-models* literature now point in opposite directions, and it is not yet decided which wins.

On the **multi-agent / debate** side, the durable references are Irving, Christiano, Amodei, *AI Safety via Debate* (2018, OpenAI; arXiv:1805.00899), which proposed debate as a scalable-oversight mechanism in which two agents argue and a weaker judge picks the winner; Khan et al., *Debating with More Persuasive LLMs Leads to More Truthful Answers* (2024, ICML; arXiv:2402.06782), which provided the first reasonably strong empirical evidence that debate improves judge accuracy under certain conditions; and the reflection / self-critique line — Shinn et al., *Reflexion* (2023, NeurIPS; arXiv:2303.11366, already in the canon at [shinn-reflexion-2023](../../canon/corpus/shinn-reflexion-2023/)), Madaan et al., *Self-Refine* (2023; arXiv:2303.17651), and Chen et al., *Devil's Advocate: Anticipatory Reflection for LLM Agents* (2024 EMNLP findings; ACL Anthology 2024.findings-emnlp.53, in the canon at [chen-devils-advocate-2024](../../canon/corpus/chen-devils-advocate-2024/)). Anthropic's [*Building Effective Agents*](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents) (2024) and [*How we built our multi-agent research system*](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system) (2025) are in the canon and articulate the *evaluator–optimizer* and *orchestrator–worker* patterns the critic-stack inherits from.

On the **judge-model** side, the literature is honest about the failure modes the critic-stack inherits silently. Zheng et al., *Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena* (2023; arXiv:2306.05685), is the canonical reference for **position bias** (judges prefer the first-presented candidate), **length bias** (judges prefer longer answers), and **self-preference bias** (judges prefer answers from the same model family). Panickssery, Bowman, Feng, *LLM Evaluators Recognize and Favor Their Own Generations* (2024; arXiv:2404.13076), tightened the self-preference result. These failure modes apply *directly* to the three-lens critic panel: the panel has the orchestrator generate the candidate (step 9), then asks three same-model lenses to judge it (step 10). If self-preference bias is real and large, the panel is structurally compromised and the operator has no instrument to detect it.

On the **inference-time-compute / reasoning-models** side — OpenAI's o-series (o1, o3, o4) and the open work that followed (Snell et al., *Scaling LLM Test-Time Compute Optimally* 2024, arXiv:2408.03314; DeepSeek-R1 2025; the broader process-reward-model line) — the implicit argument is that *the work belongs inside the model*, not in the orchestration layer. If a single model with extended thinking can produce well-critiqued reasoning, multi-agent scaffolding becomes redundant scaffolding. This is the strongest argument *against* the critic-stack as a long-term direction: each model generation may eat one tier of orchestration. The counter-argument is that orchestration provides *structural* properties (auditability, hard gates, contradicting-retrieval, frame-level objection capture) that inference-time compute alone does not provide even when it is smarter. The critic-stack's design implicitly bets on the second view; this bet should be named.

## Decision-quality and forecasting literature

The stack draws on Kahneman and Lovallo's *Timid Choices and Bold Forecasts* (1993, *Management Science*) and Flyvbjerg's reference-class-forecasting work for the outside-view discipline; both are in the canon manifest. Tetlock's *Superforecasting* (2015) and the IARPA / Good Judgment Project work behind it provide the calibration backbone — what a *trained* forecaster does that an untrained one does not. The most relevant single piece of structured-analytic-techniques literature is Heuer's *Psychology of Intelligence Analysis* (1999, CIA Center for the Study of Intelligence) and Heuer & Pherson's *Structured Analytic Techniques for Intelligence Analysis* (2nd ed., 2014) — the intelligence-community tradition has been doing red-cell, devil's-advocate, and analysis-of-competing-hypotheses work for decades, and the critic-stack reinvents fragments of this tradition without naming them.

Klein's *Sources of Power* (1998) and his pre-mortem essay (*Performing a Project Premortem*, 2007, *Harvard Business Review*) are the canonical reference for institutionalised pre-failure imagination — and pre-mortems are notable because they are *one-shot, time-boxed, oral*, not artifact-heavy. The critic-stack's frame-challenger is closer to a written pre-mortem; whether the artifact form is more or less effective than Klein's oral form is, to my knowledge, not directly studied.

The literature on why decision frameworks **fail** is older and more humbling: cargo-culting, ritualisation without belief, surface compliance vs. genuine dissent (Janis, *Groupthink*, 1972; March & Olsen, *Garbage Can Model*, 1972 — both stubs in spirit, if not in this corpus). The clearest single warning to the critic-stack from this tradition is that *adversarial* roles institutionalise into *ceremonial* roles within ~18 months unless the institution actively rotates the adversary. The Catholic Church abolished the *Promotor Fidei* ("devil's advocate") office in 1983 because, among other reasons, it had become a procedural box-tick.

## The Claude / agent tooling ecosystem

The relevant primitives the stack already uses or could use: the Claude Code subagent system (used heavily), MCP servers (used minimally — settings.json holds none), Skills (referenced in the lab as a possible future), hooks (unused), slash commands (one — `/upgrade`), Routines (referenced for `canon-refresher` scheduling, not yet wired). Sibling projects in the multi-agent space — LangGraph (graph-typed agent flows), CrewAI (role-typed agent crews), AutoGen (conversational multi-agent) — make explicit what `claude-critic-stack` keeps implicit: the *workflow shape itself* is a first-class artifact. The critic-stack's workflow lives in a prose section of [CLAUDE.md](../../CLAUDE.md); it is not declarative and cannot be re-arranged without editing prose. This is a deliberate simplicity that has costs.

Memory and longitudinal-state systems — vector stores, knowledge graphs, Anthropic's recent memory features, project-level vs. user-level state — are conspicuously absent from the stack. By design: the [.claude/session-artifacts/README.md](../../.claude/session-artifacts/README.md) is explicit that "no agent is instructed to read prior sessions." This preserves the per-session voice but forecloses every form of *learning from outcomes*, which is the single largest gap relative to the forecasting literature the stack imports its discipline from.

## Where the repo sits in this landscape

The critic-stack is **uniquely well-positioned** for: (a) holding a hard gate against ungrounded generation (the precondition-on-disk pattern is rare and good); (b) keeping an audit trail that survives the conversation (most agentic tools throw away the deliberation); (c) treating the working directory as the anchor (a structural rather than exhortatory anti-anchoring move); (d) refusing the agreeability default at the architecture layer rather than the prompt layer.

It is **uniquely poorly-positioned** for: (a) measuring whether any of this works — there is no calibration or outcome-tracking mechanism; (b) detecting self-preference bias in its own panel — same-model judges judging same-model generators is exactly the configuration the literature flags; (c) growing the canon at the rate the field is moving — the corpus is mostly stubs and the refresher is curator-gated; (d) handling clusters of related decisions — the workflow is one-question-shaped; (e) revisiting prior recommendations when new evidence lands — there is no revisitation primitive at all.

## The corpus-shape problem

Reading [canon/sources.yaml](../../canon/sources.yaml) and walking [canon/corpus/](../../canon/corpus/), three things are true at once. First, the *manifest* is broad and reasonable (distributed systems, DDD, refactoring, integration, event-driven, resilience, ADRs, testing, forecasting). Second, the *fully-ingested* subset is narrow and Anthropic-heavy. Third, the topics the manifest itself flags as *not yet covered* — frontend architecture, security engineering, data-platform / lakehouse, AI/ML system design, organisational (Team Topologies, Accelerate) — are exactly the topics the critic-stack would need to handle if used outside back-end-systems decisions. The librarian's "thin coverage" output is therefore the *honest* output for any question outside back-end systems and forecasting; the stack does not currently advertise this in its README.

These landscape facts shape the long list and the deep dives that follow.
