# Frame challenges — `2026-04-27-critics-get-write-tool-impl`

## Frame as given

The orchestrator's working frame (Revision 1) is **orchestrator parsimony, bounded by write-target boundary**: the question is reframed as "what orchestrator paraphrase work disappears when critics write their own verdicts, and what new boundary-enforcement work appears in its place?" The user's audit-faithfulness motive is preserved as a sub-goal — it is the *reason* the new shape pays its boundary cost. Alt-2 (boundary) is named as the constraint; Alt-3 (recursion) is acknowledged but not adopted as the working frame.

## Alternative frame

**The critic-panel is a *voting* system, not a *recording* system, and this proposal is a vote-tampering risk before it is a transcription-fidelity question.** Under this framing, the verb is not *grant-write* and not *reduce-orchestrator-paraphrase* — the verb is *isolate-the-ballot*. The optimization target shifts from "the verdict the operator reads matches the verdict the model produced" (audit-faithfulness) to "no critic can be influenced by, or appear to have been influenced by, any other critic's verdict before its own is final." Today's prose-return-to-orchestrator architecture happens to enforce ballot isolation accidentally — the orchestrator is a pure aggregator that cannot leak one critic's content into another critic's context window because the critics return in parallel and the orchestrator does not re-prompt them with each other's output. Granting `Write` to three agents with overlapping path conventions in a session that has parallel invocations introduces a TOCTOU/visibility surface the prose-return shape did not have: a critic invoked second-in-wallclock can in principle `Read` `critiques/architecture.md` while writing `critiques/operations.md`. That risk is not on the current frame's threat list at all — the frame is preoccupied with "did the orchestrator paraphrase faithfully" when canon (Building Effective Agents §Voting, plus the Google SRE Admin Server passage on independent audit) says the load-bearing property is *vote independence*, not *transcription fidelity*. Different verb (isolate, not record), different downstream answer (e.g., critics get `Write` only via a harness that enforces no-`Read` on sibling critic files within the same session, or critics return prose and the *orchestrator* is the only writer to `critiques/<lens>.md` after-the-fact — preserving today's accidental isolation while addressing paraphrase loss with a structured-return schema instead of a tool grant).

## Condition under which the current frame is wrong

The frame is wrong if any of the three critics, in any session, ever has reason or capability to `Read` a sibling critic's `critiques/<lens>.md` before its own write completes — at that point ballot independence is broken, and the audit-faithfulness gain is dwarfed by a vote-collusion loss the current frame does not consider.

## Challenges to scope-map preservations

- **Upgrade #3 (PostToolUse hook to verify claimed writes — preserved as un-built):** preservation reason was "natural complement, not hard dependency; instruction-level scoping is sufficient under the established pattern; coupling this requirement to an un-built profound-tagged primitive would expand scope unacceptably." That reason is weak because the outside-view distillation explicitly names "stale-session cross-contamination / wrong-path writes" as the **modal failure mode** of this proposal (§5, claim #5) and rates the proposal *below base rate* (~40–50%) for clean transition specifically because the load-bearing artifacts — including a session-id propagation contract with write-time assertion — are missing. Preserving #3 as un-built means shipping a change whose own outside-view forecast says it will fail at base rate without the very hook #3 would build. The framing "instruction-level scoping is sufficient *because the existing Write-holders use instruction-level scoping*" is a precedent argument, not a risk argument: the existing Write-holders are `subagent-distiller`, `scope-mapper`, and `frame-challenger` — all of which run **once, sequentially, with the orchestrator's full attention on a single returned artifact**. The critics run **three-in-parallel**, which is the exact concurrency surface #3 was designed for. The preservation biases the downstream answer toward **"ship it, monitor in production"** when the canon-librarian's Anthropic-poka-yoke passage (Building Effective Agents Appendix 2) and the outside-view's TrueFoundry / SEI CERT references both say poka-yoke before deployment is cheaper than detection after. A defensible alternative to preservation: name #3 as a **co-shipping prerequisite for the parallel-write case specifically**, even if instruction-level remains the policy for sequential Write-holders.

- **Upgrade #14 (hard gates as harness hooks — preserved as un-built):** preservation reason was "orthogonal; this requirement does not invoke the hard-gate mechanism; gate enforcement remains prose-level." That reason is weak because the orthogonality claim treats #14 only as a Step-9-gate concern (the existing `scope-map.md` + `challenges.md` precondition), but the *category* of #14 is "moving load-bearing rules from prose to harness," and this requirement is itself a candidate for that move — the rule "critic writes go to `<id>/critiques/<lens>.md` and nowhere else" is exactly the prose-level load-bearing rule that #14's pattern would lift into harness. Calling them orthogonal is a category error: they share a target. The preservation biases the downstream answer toward **"prose is fine because prose has been fine"** — a status-quo bias the canon-librarian's "maintain simplicity *and* prioritize transparency" pair (Building Effective Agents §Summary) actually cuts both ways on, and which the outside-view's "visible bounded loss vs. potentially invisible unbounded loss" framing (§5, claim #8) directly contradicts. A defensible alternative to preservation: name #14 as the **mechanism by which #3 would be implemented**, treat the two as a single co-shipping bundle, and decide whether *that bundle* blocks this requirement or runs alongside it — rather than dismissing both as orthogonal in isolation.

## Challenge to the classifier label

The classifier's primary label was `extend` with `replace` as the secondary on the aggregate-`critiques.md` arm; the recursive-frame note already flagged that critics tend to approve self-improvements that expand their own surface. The substantive alternative is **`new`**, not `refactor`: this is not "the same critic with one more capability" — it is the introduction of a *new contract* in which the critic is **simultaneously** a reviewer and an artifact-author, two responsibilities the current design separates. Under the `new` framing, the downstream frame would change in two ways. First, the question "should we add `Write`" becomes "should the role *critic-as-reviewer* and the role *critic-as-author-of-record* be the same agent at all" — which the canon-librarian's Building Effective Agents §Summary (loose coupling, single-responsibility ACI) and the SRE Ch. 7 Admin Server passage (auditor-with-trail is a separate party from actor) both push back on. Second, the `extend` framing licenses the user's "instruction-level scoping is sufficient" assumption by analogy to `subagent-distiller` et al.; the `new` framing breaks that analogy because a new role with a new responsibility cannot inherit the old role's permission discipline by precedent. The orchestrator's current frame uses `extend`-shaped reasoning ("apply the same instruction-level scoping pattern") on what is structurally a `new`-shaped change.

## Defense against critic-panel rubber-stamping (recursion concern)

The current frame **does not have a defense against rubber-stamping**, and Revision 1 explicitly demoted Alt-3 (recursion / self-modification hygiene) from working-frame to acknowledged-but-not-adopted. The recursive-frame note in `requirement.md` and the outside-view §5 claim #7 ("the critic-panel may lack a lens named 'audit-integrity' or 'tool-permission-blast-radius' that would catch this category of change") together name the gap; the frame does not close it. The three available defenses, in increasing strength:

1. **Operator-override gate.** Step 10's `approve` verdicts on this specific session are non-binding — the operator must explicitly accept the panel's approval before Step 12 runs. Cheap, but only a gate; does not change critic incentives.
2. **Replan-on-self-modification rule.** Append to Step 11: any change whose scope-map touches an agent file under `.claude/agents/critic-*.md` auto-routes to a frame-level replan regardless of panel verdict. Forces the critics to argue against an *already-skeptical* orchestrator rather than the other way around.
3. **External second-opinion run.** The proposed `shadow-comparator` from a separate upgrade (referenced in `frame.md` Alt-3) runs the same panel under the *current* (pre-change) critic configuration as a control. Strongest, but presumes the un-built primitive exists.

Without one of these, the panel's verdict on this change is structurally weaker evidence than its verdict on a non-self-modifying change, and the synthesis step (12) should weight it accordingly. The frame as written gives the synthesis step no instruction to do so. **The generator must either adopt one of the three defenses above, or explicitly state that it is shipping under panel-approval-with-known-recursion-bias and name the discount factor.**

## Frame-level objection the orchestrator should carry forward

The audit-faithfulness gain this proposal pays for is small and bounded; the ballot-independence and self-modification-rubber-stamping risks it introduces are each potentially larger and unbounded — and the current frame names neither as a load-bearing constraint.

## Loop 2 challenges

### Frame as given (Revision 2)

The orchestrator's working frame is now **parliament-with-clerk separation, optimizing for vote-independence-and-byte-fidelity**. The verb is *separate clerk from voter*; the question is "given that vote-independence and byte-fidelity are both load-bearing, what is the cheapest shape that delivers both, and where does the work of recording go?" Two candidate shapes are pre-named (v2-A: structured-return + orchestrator-verbatim-write; v2-B: dedicated `recorder` agent), and the original tool-grant candidate is rejected on frame.

### Alternative frame to parliament-with-clerk

**The critic-panel is a *judging panel issuing opinions*, not a parliament casting votes — and the artifact in question is a *signed opinion*, not a vote-record.** A parliament's load-bearing property is vote-independence-before-tally; a judging panel's load-bearing property is *each opinion being attributable to its author with non-repudiation*, with independence being a means to that end rather than the end itself. Under the opinion frame, the verb is *attribute-and-sign*, not *isolate-and-record*. This reframes the candidate space: the failure mode is not "voter B sees voter A's ballot" (a parliament concern) but "the operator cannot tell, three sessions later, whether `critiques/architecture.md` is what the architecture critic actually said or what the orchestrator transcribed *from* what the architecture critic said." Under the opinion frame, **v2-A (structured-return + orchestrator-verbatim-write) is the *worse* answer**, not the cheaper one — verbatim-by-mechanical-copy is structurally indistinguishable from paraphrase to a downstream reader unless the harness signs the copy with provenance metadata, which neither v2-A nor v2-B currently propose. The parliament frame treats authorship as a means (vote-independence is what we want; clerk-separation is the mechanism); the opinion frame treats authorship as the end (operator non-repudiation is what we want; isolation is one of several mechanisms). The user's original framing words — "no more me-claiming-they-said-X" — are closer to the opinion frame than to the parliament frame. The parliament frame may be over-reading the architecture lens's role-fusion language and under-reading the user's stated motive.

### Condition under which the parliament-with-clerk frame is wrong

The parliament-with-clerk frame is wrong if vote-independence is **not actually load-bearing for this stack's use case** — specifically, if the three critics' verdicts already correlate strongly through shared context (same session, same candidate, same upstream artifacts they all read) such that the marginal independence-loss from filesystem-mediated sibling-visibility is dominated by the independence-loss already present in the prompt. If three critics reading the same `requirement.md`, the same `frame.md`, and the same generator output already produce ~70% verdict-correlation by construction (because they share inputs), then a TOCTOU window in which one might glimpse another's output adds bounded marginal correlation to an already-correlated set — and the cost of clerk-separation (one more agent, more orchestration, deferred byte-fidelity) buys vanishingly little. The parliament metaphor presumes voters who would vote independently absent contamination; if the voters are already three forks of the same context, the metaphor's load-bearing property is fictional.

### Is the candidate space really {v2-A, v2-B}?

**No, and the orchestrator's narrowing to two candidates is itself a frame artifact worth interrogating.** A third viable shape, not collusion with either:

**v2-C: Critics return prose; orchestrator writes per-lens files; *no aggregate, no thin index, no schema* — the per-lens file is the operator-facing surface and Step 12 reads them directly.** This shape preserves clerk-voter separation (orchestrator is the sole writer, critics never touch Write), preserves vote-independence by construction (no filesystem path between critics — same property as today), and addresses the user's audit-faithfulness motive via a *byte-fidelity contract on the orchestrator* ("when the orchestrator persists a critic's return, the bytes between the agreed delimiters are copied verbatim; the orchestrator may not paraphrase, summarize, or reformat"). It rejects v2-A's structured-return (which forces the critic to render its prose into a typed schema that is itself a paraphrase pressure) and rejects v2-B's recorder agent (which adds an agent and serial work for no independence gain over v2-C). It rejects the assumption — shared by both v2-A and v2-B — that the *agent* is the right place to enforce the contract; in v2-C, the contract is **on CLAUDE.md prose binding the orchestrator's transcription behavior**, which is the chokepoint that already exists. The reason v2-C is not in the orchestrator's candidate set is that Revision 2's frame ("vote-independence-and-byte-fidelity") subtly assumes byte-fidelity must be delivered by the *author* (critic-as-self-writer) or by a *mechanical copy from a typed object* (structured-return), when in fact byte-fidelity can be delivered by **a prose contract on the existing single writer** — the cheapest shape, and the one closest to the current working architecture.

A fourth shape worth naming, weaker but real:

**v2-D: Do nothing; reject the user's motive.** The user's stated pain ("no more me-claiming-they-said-X") presumes the orchestrator's paraphrase is *currently* a meaningful audit problem. The session record does not actually establish this — there is no audit incident on file in which an orchestrator paraphrase was found to mis-represent a critic's verdict. The motive is hypothetical-bias prevention. v2-D says: until there is a measured incident, the cheapest answer is no change. Both v2-A and v2-B (and v2-C) are paying complexity costs to mitigate an unmeasured failure mode; v2-D is the null hypothesis the orchestrator has not entertained because it conflicts with the user's framing words. The frame-challenger's job is to entertain it.

### Convergence-as-replan-signal: was loop 1 actually convergence, or three lenses reaching for the strongest available objection?

The orchestrator's loop 1 routing decision rests on the claim that three lenses arrived at the *same* structural complaint from independent angles — and per upgrade #14b, that convergence is the replan signal. This claim deserves harder scrutiny than `critiques.md` gave it.

**Reasons to suspect the convergence is partly an artifact of shared-vocabulary, not shared-insight:**

1. The architecture lens explicitly named "role-fusion / parliament-vs-pipeline." The operations and product lenses ran *after* (or in parallel against) a candidate that had already named "Defense 2: replan-on-self-modification" — a defense whose existence telegraphs the role-fusion concern as the most-available objection. Both lenses may have reached for the strongest objection in the *candidate's own threat surface* rather than independently surfacing the same insight. Convergence-via-shared-prompt is not the same as convergence-via-independent-discovery.

2. The three "convergent restatements" in `critiques.md` (architecture: voter ≠ clerk; operations: parallel ≠ sequential; product: operator-stakeholder unnamed) are restated in parliament vocabulary *by the orchestrator* in the aggregate file. Reading the per-lens files directly: operations' actual frame objection is **concurrency-as-step-change**, which is orthogonal to clerk-voter separation (a sequential dedicated-recorder agent satisfies operations' concern, and so does v2-C with no recorder at all). Product's actual frame objection is **operator-not-named-as-stakeholder**, which is orthogonal to both (v2-A satisfies neither concern; v2-C satisfies product's concern by making the per-lens file the operator surface, but not by clerk-separation). The orchestrator's "convergent on parliament" framing is a *post-hoc unification* that compresses three different objections into one. The three objections do converge on "the candidate's frame is wrong," but they do not converge on *which alternative frame is right*.

3. Architecture's own self-disclosure in critiques/architecture.md §Note: "I am rejecting; that direction of bias (rejecting a change to my own surface) is the *opposite* of the rubber-stamping bias the recursion concern names, but it is also a bias — toward status-quo on self-modification." Architecture explicitly invited the orchestrator to discount its verdict if the other two lenses *did not* converge from independent angles. The other two converged on different angles, and the orchestrator collapsed them into architecture's angle. That collapse is the move architecture asked the orchestrator *not* to make.

**Implication for loop 2:** the parliament-with-clerk frame may be over-fitted to architecture's vocabulary. Operations would be satisfied by any shape that resolves concurrency posture (canary, write-time session-id assertion, single-writer); product would be satisfied by any shape that names the operator and pins a schema. Neither *requires* clerk-voter separation. The orchestrator should not treat Revision 2's frame as the only frame that survives the loop 1 critique — v2-C (orchestrator-as-sole-writer with byte-fidelity prose contract) addresses operations and product directly without adopting parliament metaphor at all.

### Frame to escalate to operator rather than synthesize

Loop 2 of 2. After loop 2's panel, synthesis must run. There is one frame the orchestrator should consider escalating to the operator before synthesis rather than synthesizing through:

**The recursion frame: a panel modifying its own review apparatus is, by construction, weaker evidence than the same panel reviewing any other change.** No amount of looping inside the panel resolves this — the panel's verdicts are discounted regardless of what shape the candidate takes. If loop 2's panel approves any of {v2-A, v2-B, v2-C, v2-D}, the operator should be told: "panel approved, but this approval is on a self-modifying change; the discount factor is not zero and the panel itself cannot tell you what it is." If loop 2's panel rejects again, the operator should be told: "panel rejected twice; the rejection may be status-quo bias on self-modification (architecture lens disclosed this in loop 1); the operator must adjudicate." Either outcome is operator-decision-shaped, not orchestrator-synthesis-shaped, because the synthesis step has no calibrated way to weight a panel verdict against the panel's own admission that its incentive surface is contaminated. The orchestrator should escalate this *as the frame*, not as one of three named uncertainties at the end of synthesis. Naming it among the uncertainties is the form of synthesis that elides it; naming it as the escalation is the form that respects it.

### Frame-level objection the orchestrator should carry forward into loop 2 generator

**Revision 2's frame may be over-fitted to architecture's vocabulary at the cost of operations' and product's actual objections; v2-A and v2-B are not the only shapes that survive loop 1, and v2-C (orchestrator-as-sole-writer with byte-fidelity prose contract) is the shape the orchestrator's narrowing has hidden.** The loop 2 generator must address whether the parliament metaphor is load-bearing or whether it is post-hoc unification, and must explicitly compare against v2-C (and acknowledge v2-D as the null hypothesis) before recommending v2-A or v2-B.
