# Challenges — AU Banking KB SOTA

## Frame as given
The orchestrator's reframe rejected the user's "comprehensive single silo" formulation and routed to four candidate frames: agent's tool ecosystem (A), live pipeline (B), query-shaped corpus (C), provenance-first (D). The implicit meta-frame across all four is *"we are designing a retrieval architecture for an AU-banking AI agent"* with optimization targets pivoting from coverage to precision-on-real-queries + auditable provenance + freshness, bounded by run-time cost.

## Alternative frame (distinct from A–D)

**Frame E — Liability-allocation architecture, not retrieval architecture.** In Australian financial services, the binding constraint is not "did the agent retrieve the right document" but *"who is on the hook when the agent's answer is wrong."* That is a question about the answer's legal status (general advice vs. personal advice under Corporations Act s766B; ASIC RG 36/RG 244/RG 271; AFSL coverage), the audit trail, the disclaimer surface, and the supersession-resolution rule when two valid documents conflict. Under this frame the artifact is a *defensible answer chain* with a regulator-facing audit log; retrieval is a downstream implementation detail. The verb shifts from "find" to "underwrite." Frames A–D all assume the retrieval/answer surface is the deliverable; Frame E says the deliverable is a controls regime that happens to use retrieval.

A second frame worth naming briefly: **Frame F — No KB; thin agent over a few canonical APIs and a citation contract.** Distinct from Frame B (which still builds a pipeline) — this says the KB itself is a category error: the agent calls APRA Connect / ASIC / Federal Register / bank product APIs at read time, caches nothing semantically, and the only persistent artifact is the citation/version-anchoring schema (#15). This is conflict #12 promoted to a frame.

## Condition under which the current frame (across A–D) is wrong
If the consuming agent's actual job is *advice that crosses the personal-advice threshold* (or AFSL-covered conduct), all four frames are wrong because retrieval quality is dominated by liability gating: the right answer to most queries is "decline + escalate," and architecture work on retrieval shape is misallocated effort relative to the answer-disposition policy.

## Challenges to scope-map open conflicts

- **#5 GraphRAG vs hybrid — close it, do not hold.** Holding this open is a punt; it is closeable today by inspecting any 50-query sample of representative AU-banking agent traffic and counting multi-hop / supersession-chain queries. The decision rule is mechanical: <20% multi-hop → hybrid only; ≥20% → graph layer over hybrid. The scope-mapper kept it open because the consuming agent is unnamed; that is the actual decision to force, and it is not a GraphRAG decision. Holding it open biases toward GraphRAG-by-default in step 9 because the lit splits roughly 50/50 and indecision favours the more elaborate option.

- **#10 Agentic retrieval vs cost — hold open, but reframe.** Keeping this open is correct *only* if the consuming-agent identity is genuinely undecided. If it is undecided, the right artifact is a 2x2 (consumer-Q&A vs. compliance-officer) × (static vs. agentic) with the cost envelope filled in for each cell, not a deferred decision. Currently it is just deferred, which biases step 9 toward agentic (because the canon voice is one-sided pro-agentic and the cost objection is abstract).

- **#11 Filesystem-as-store vs vector index — close it: reject filesystem for this corpus.** The Anthropic stance generalizes from a *coding* corpus (one tree, stable paths, monotonic edits, defined-term-light). A 10⁶-page AU regulator corpus is graph-shaped (cross-references, supersessions, jurisdictional overlays, defined-term webs) and multi-publisher (no canonical tree). Holding this open invites a step-9 cargo-culted "Claude Code did it" recommendation. Close as: filesystem is a useful *secondary* surface for raw artifact retrieval, not the primary index.

- **#12 Live federation vs built KB — cannot close without facts the orchestrator does not have, but reshape the question.** The decision hinges on three empirical facts (API availability, rate-limit tolerance, point-in-time queryability of APRA/ASIC/AUSTRAC/FRL). The right move is not to hold a yes/no conflict open but to flag this as a *required Explore step* before generation — a five-line probe of the four publishers' API capabilities. Holding it open as architectural conflict biases step 9 toward "build" (default reflex); converting it to a probe surfaces that the answer may already exist.

- **#13 Vendor regtech vs build — close partially: assume hybrid.** Build-vs-buy is rarely all-or-nothing in regtech; the realistic answer is *vendor corpus for licensed instruments + thin in-house layer for citation/eval/refresh + bank's own product policies*. Holding the binary open lets step 9 either (a) ignore vendors entirely (LLM-default reflex toward "build") or (b) overweight them. Close as: vendor licensing is a procurement question parallel to the architecture, not an architectural either/or.

## Challenge to the orchestrator's meta-frame
The reframe quietly substituted *"retrieval architecture"* for the user's *"knowledge silo."* That substitution is load-bearing and under-argued. The user's silo framing is *more correct* under at least these conditions: (a) the consuming "agent" is in fact a heterogeneous population of future agents whose query shapes are unknown — in which case a stable, well-curated, provenance-rich corpus is the durable artifact and the retrieval layer is replaceable; (b) the political/procurement reality is that "we built a retrieval router" does not get funded but "we built the bank's authoritative AI knowledge base" does — silo framing is then the correct organizational artifact even if it is the wrong technical one; (c) regulator inspection: ARPA/ASIC asking "what is the source of truth your AI uses" expects an answerable noun, not a router. The orchestrator's reframe is biased toward the technically-fashionable answer (agent-shaped retrieval surface) and away from the artifact the bank can actually point at and defend.

## Frame that invalidates the 12-step workflow's premise
**The user may be asking a literature-survey question, not a design question.** "What is the current SOTA way" is phrased as a research request. If the user wants a *map of the field* (what 2026 practice looks like, with named approaches and their tradeoffs) before they commit to designing anything, then steps 7–11 (scope-map, frame-challenge, generator, critic-panel) are solving the wrong problem — the right output is an annotated landscape from canon-librarian + outside-view, with the user choosing a frame *afterward*. The whole gather-then-recommend pipeline assumes a decision is being made now; if the user is in pre-decision exploration, the recommendation step manufactures false confidence.

## Frame-level objection the orchestrator must carry forward
The recommendation must justify why retrieval architecture is the right deliverable at all, given (a) the unnamed consuming agent, (b) the unanswered live-API question, and (c) the possibility that the user wanted a field map rather than a design.
