# Frame challenges — item 7 (residual AGENTS.md references)

## Frame as given

The orchestrator reframes item 7 from "scrub a deleted filename" to "audit the four jobs the string `AGENTS.md` is doing in the corpus, kill the three that have no referent, preserve the one that documents history." The frame partitions occurrences into prescriptive/structural (Jobs 1–3, replace) and narrative/historical (Job 4, extend with forward-pointer). The optimization axis is *narrative vs. prescriptive*: prescriptive prose with a dead referent is broken and must be rewritten; narrative prose about a past state is dated and must be preserved. The frame is bounded by an explicit out-of-scope: "Restore AGENTS.md is not on the table."

## Alternative frame

The right axis is not *narrative vs. prescriptive* — it is **dated vs. live**. Every occurrence in the corpus, including the upgrade-entry "🚀 upgrade" benefit cells the current frame wants to rewrite, is *dated*: each upgrade README carries a YYYY-MM-DD slug, was authored at a specific moment, and records what its author *then* believed the benefit to be. Under the dated/live axis, an upgrade entry's benefit cell is not a live prescription the repo must keep true — it is a frozen claim about what the author at that date thought the upgrade would buy, exactly the same epistemic class as a catalyst's grep finding. The catalyst says "at time T, AGENTS.md was named in zero references to upgrades/"; the benefit cell says "at time T, the author believed the upgrade would make AGENTS.md/CLAUDE.md drift detectable." Both are dated authorial claims; both become epistemically odd when later edited under updated knowledge. Under this frame, the verb for the benefit cells is not *replace* but *annotate-and-preserve* (strikethrough plus a footnote noting the parity premise lost a leg when AGENTS.md was retired) — symmetric with the Job-4 treatment. The optimization target shifts from *prescription integrity* to *authorial-record integrity across the upgrade canon*.

## Condition under which the current frame is wrong

The current frame is wrong if any future reader will read the upgrade-entry benefit cells as *historical evidence of what the author then claimed* (e.g., during meta-review, supersession analysis, or drift audits) rather than as *live promises the repo must keep true* — because rewriting the cell under current knowledge destroys the dated record that meta-review depends on, and there is no separate place in the repo (no ADR Superseded-by header, no upgrade-entry "premise revised" marker) that recovers what the original cell said.

## Challenges to scope-map preservations

- **Catalyst sections at `workflow-blind-to-the-lab/README.md:9` and `:38` (preserved as `extend`):** the stated reason is that catalysts are dated diagnostic findings and rewriting them is retconning history under current knowledge, anchored to the user's explicit "Catalyst sections describing the past can stay" and to the SRE postmortem-culture canon passage. **The reason is weak because** the same logic applies — without modification — to the Job-2 benefit cells the scope-map sends to `replace`. A benefit cell authored 2026-04-26 stating *"CLAUDE.md/AGENTS.md drift becomes detectable"* is no less a dated authorial record than a catalyst grep authored the same week. The scope-map's own "counter-pressure named" paragraph admits this explicitly — "upgrade-entry cells are also dated records … retiring/marking benefit cells itself contaminates the historical record" — and then defers the resolution to the generator without resolving the asymmetry. Preserving catalysts while rewriting benefit cells encodes a hidden axiom: that *narrative* prose is sacred but *argumentative* prose is fungible. **The preservation biases the downstream answer toward** treating the catalyst as a unique, almost-museum-quality artifact while implicitly denying the same status to the rest of the upgrade entry's body — which licenses the generator to rewrite five argumentative cells without flagging that each rewrite is a small act of historical revision. A symmetric frame would either preserve all five with strikethrough/footnote, or rewrite all five including the catalyst — choosing one rule and applying it across.

- **Session-artifacts immutability convention (preserved as `extend`):** the stated reason is that the convention is the load-bearing reason this whole item is bounded — editing the convention would be circular. **The reason is weak because** "we cannot edit the convention because the convention bounds the item" is true but trivial; the substantive question is whether the convention's current scope (session-artifacts only) is *sufficient* for item 7's needs, or whether item 7 is exposing a gap — namely, that the repo has no convention covering *upgrade-entry bodies* (which are quasi-immutable: 🔖-committed, lifecycle-marked, but apparently editable when a referenced doc disappears). Preserving the session-artifacts convention as-is silently endorses the position that upgrade-entry bodies are *not* peer-class to session artifacts. **The preservation biases the downstream answer toward** treating upgrade entries as live documents subject to ongoing editorial revision, rather than as dated records that should follow the same immutability discipline as session artifacts. If they should, the right resolution is a new convention (an upgrade-entry equivalent of "supersede, don't edit"), not five surgical cell rewrites.

- **Punch-list ✅ pattern and upgrade-entry lifecycle markers (preserved as `extend`):** the stated reason is that they are meta-conventions, not targets of the requirement. **The reason is weak because** editing a 🔖-committed upgrade entry's body cells is not obviously compatible with the lifecycle-marker convention's *intent* — the marker advertises a settled state, and silent body edits violate that advertisement without advancing the marker. The scope-map flags this as "decide *how* to honor it" and punts to the generator. **The preservation biases the downstream answer toward** assuming lifecycle markers are decorative (they survive any body edit) rather than load-bearing (a body edit invalidates or advances the marker). If the markers are load-bearing, item 7 cannot legitimately edit a 🔖 entry's body without either reverting the marker or inventing a "premise-revised" marker — neither of which is currently in scope.

## Challenge to the classifier label

The classifier chose `refactor` with a self-flagged bias toward calling a scope-shrink a refactor, and named `investigation` as the alternative classification (a per-occurrence categorization without edits). The alternative classification is substantively different and the framing words *"refactor of language"* and *"action prose ... needs to be revised"* embed the bias the classifier warned about. Under `investigation`, the deliverable is the categorization table itself — narrative/prescriptive, dated/live, load-bearing/decorative — *and a decision about whether the upgrade canon needs a new convention (supersede-don't-edit) before any cell is touched*. The downstream frame would shift from "find the right verb per occurrence and apply it" to "answer the prior question of whether upgrade-entry bodies are mutable at all, then act." This matters because the current frame inherits from `refactor` an implicit answer (yes, mutable, surgically) that has not been argued for and that the symmetry challenge above directly contests.

## Frame-level objection the orchestrator should carry forward

The frame's narrative/prescriptive split is doing the work of the dated/live split it should be doing — generating the recommendation must either justify why upgrade-entry benefit cells are *less dated* than catalysts, or apply a single rule symmetrically across both, or invoke a supersede-don't-edit convention that does not yet exist in the repo and acknowledge that inventing it is in scope.
