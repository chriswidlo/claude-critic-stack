# Frame challenges — path-discipline regime

## Frame as given

The orchestrator's `frame.md` Revision 1 reframes the question as: *given that the rule was added for grep-stability + privacy and that 9-vs-9 signals adoption failure, choose between (i) preserve + fix enforcement, (ii) replace style, (iii) split by surface, or (iv) replace prose with tooling — and verify the choice doesn't degrade the longitudinal-record property.* The frame still treats "a rule about path style exists in CLAUDE.md" as load-bearing; every option keeps a CLAUDE.md clause. The question being asked is *which clause text*, not *whether the clause should be there*.

## Alternative frame — the rule is mis-located, not mis-worded

The CLAUDE.md path-discipline clause is in the wrong substrate. CLAUDE.md is the orchestrator's behavioral prompt — a place to encode *what to do when answering design questions*. Path style is an authoring-output convention. Mixing them means every design-question session pays prompt-budget tax for a convention that should live where paths are *written* (the slash command, the three Write-capable agents, and a check script), not where decisions are *made* (the orchestrator prompt). Reframed: *"the question isn't A/B/C on clause 2 — it's whether clause 2 belongs in CLAUDE.md at all, or whether removing it from CLAUDE.md and pushing the constraint down to the four authoring surfaces (3 agents + 1 slash command) is the actual structural fix."* Under this frame, the 9-vs-9 split is not adoption failure of a rule — it is correct behavior of agents who never read CLAUDE.md (per Explore §4: zero of ten agents reference it). Authors don't drift from a rule they never received; they default. The rule's location is the bug.

This is *different* from `frame.md` R1.3 (tooling-vs-prose) and R1.2 (split-by-surface). R1.3 keeps the rule in CLAUDE.md and adds a hook; R1.2 keeps the rule in CLAUDE.md and forks it. This frame says CLAUDE.md should not carry an authoring-output rule at all — it carries a decision-process rule. The path-style constraint is a co-tenant that doesn't belong.

## Condition under which the current frame is wrong

**The current frame is wrong if the actual long-run reader of session artifacts is `grep` invoked by the orchestrator, and not a human or GitHub renderer.** If the dominant consumer is mechanical text-search across the artifact corpus, then *both* link styles are equivalent (grep matches the literal string either way) and the entire A/B/C dispute collapses — the right answer is "pick one and stop talking about it; the consumer doesn't care." The frame presupposes that the choice has consequences for some reader; if no such reader exists in practice, the question is mis-scoped as a *decision* and is actually a *taste* question being inflated by attachment.

## Challenges to scope-map preservations

**Longitudinal-record contract (commit `5108ed3` — session artifacts immutable except for privacy).** Stated reason: operator declared it explicitly + commit carries it as a quoted rule. Weak because the contract was authored by the same operator who is now using it as a justification not to touch the two file-relative session-artifact violators — the contract's *scope* (which sweeps count as "style" vs. "privacy") was never adjudicated against a real test case, and this question is the first one. Treating an untested contract as preserved means whatever scope it implicitly takes here becomes precedent. The preservation biases the downstream answer toward *"any option that requires touching past artifacts is off the table,"* which pre-rules out a real Option D (canonicalize the past, then enforce mechanically going forward) without it ever being weighed. The contract deserves to be applied, but the boundary it draws here — "style fixes are not privacy fixes" — is the operator's call, not a quoted rule's call.

**Reasons (a) privacy and (d) consent boundary, preserved without question.** Stated reason: zero leak hits in the Explore §7 audit; uncontested by the operator. Weak because the audit measured *current state*, not *counterfactual breach rate without the rule*. The privacy clause may be doing nothing — agents may simply never produce absolute paths in 2026-04 because Claude Code's tool surface (`Read`/`Write` accept absolute paths but agents are prompted with workspace-relative context) makes the failure mode rare independent of the rule. Reason (d) is similarly unaudited: the consent boundary on outside-repo references is real, but no measurement shows agents *would* quote outside-repo paths absent the clause. The preservation biases toward *"any reduction of CLAUDE.md path text is unsafe,"* which inflates Option A's apparent conservatism. If (a) and (d) are over-protective relative to current tooling, then collapsing them into a one-line `no absolute paths, no outside-repo quoting` directive — and dropping clause 2 entirely — becomes a real option the scope-map did not surface.

**The "non-negotiable" status of the rule itself.** Stated reason: the operator's framing word, noted in `requirement.md` but flagged as not load-bearing. Weak because "non-negotiable" was authored by the same operator now asking whether to negotiate it; the word is doing rhetorical work — it pre-commits the operator against Option B before the analysis runs. Calling a rule non-negotiable while running a frame-challenger on it is a tell: either the word is wrong (the rule is in fact negotiable, and saying otherwise is anchoring) or the workflow is wrong (running the stack on something that's actually settled). The preservation of "non-negotiable" status biases toward A by making B feel like a violation of operator intent rather than a legitimate option. The frame should treat the word as evidence of attachment, not as a constraint.

## Condition that flips Option A → Option B

**Signal that invalidates the "medium" confidence on A and forces B:** if a measurement of the next 14 days of agent-authored output shows that adding the path-discipline directive to the slash command and the three Write-capable agents produces *less than 80% repo-root compliance* in newly-created files (i.e., the directive does not, in fact, change agent behavior). The plan's central premise is that "enforcement-surface silence" causes the drift (Explore §Synthesis); A is recommended because patching the surfaces is assumed sufficient. If a 14-day post-patch audit shows agents still default to file-relative — because the directive is being summarized away in long-context turns, or because the agents are pattern-matching on the surrounding subdir context faster than they read the directive — then the prose-fix-without-mechanical-gate hypothesis is falsified, and Option B (flip to the style agents naturally produce) becomes the lower-friction path. The outside-view distillation forecasts ~35% for A; an empirical 14-day compliance number under 80% is the operationalization of "below base rate" for *this* repo.

## Adversarial frame on the slash-command-patch fix

The plan calls "patch the slash command" structural. It is not. A slash-command patch is a prose directive in a prompt — it relies on the model reading, retaining, and applying the directive at every write. The outside-view distillation says exactly this: A is below base rate *unless* a non-optional mechanical gate exists, and a slash-command directive is not a mechanical gate. The canon-librarian (passages 1–4) is unambiguous: poka-yoke is changing the *tool* so mistakes are harder, and the SRE passage on automation says the value of consistency comes from removing the human (or model) from the procedure. A slash-command sentence does not remove the model; it adds a sentence the model may or may not weight. Calling that "structural" is rhetorical inflation — it is *prose enforcement of prose*, dressed up by being located in a different prose file. The actual structural fix is one of: a `bin/check-path-discipline.sh` invoked by a pre-commit hook; an agent-side validator that reads candidate output and rejects file-relative links before the Write tool is called; or removing path style as a decision by templating link generation. Anything short of those is soft. The plan's own confidence wording ("medium") is a tell that the operator suspects this. The frame-challenger's call: "patch the slash command" is a soft fix dressed as structural, and the recommendation should not count it as the load-bearing piece — the load-bearing piece is the optional step 6 (the bin script), and A's viability stands or falls on whether step 6 is mandatory.

## Honesty meta-challenge

The orchestrator (the operator) authored `plans/2026-04-27-repo-cleanup-punch-list.md` with file-relative links, in violation of the rule the operator is now recommending be preserved. The plan being evaluated then recommends Option A — keep the rule. This is the textbook shape of post-hoc rationalization: the artifact-of-violation precedes the decision-to-preserve, and the decision-to-preserve protects the violator from having to be re-litigated. Counterfactual test: had the operator written the punch list compliantly the first time, would A still be recommended? Probably yes — but the *confidence* would not be "medium," it would be lower, because the absence of a recent personal violation would reduce the cost of admitting the rule is too costly to follow. The "medium" confidence is partly a reflection of the operator's own friction with the rule, surfaced by their own non-compliance, and the recommendation to keep the rule is partly a way to avoid having to say *"I drifted because the rule is wrong."* The honest read: the punch-list violation is data, not noise. It is one of the 9 file-relative cases, authored by the most rule-aware writer in the repo, and the operator's response is to fix the artifact rather than update the rule. That is a legitimate choice — but it is the choice that requires the most justification, and the plan does not name it. The frame-challenger's call: the operator should explicitly state *"I violated the rule and I am choosing to fix the artifact rather than the rule, for these reasons"* — and if those reasons cannot be written down without circularity (the rule is right because it is the rule), Option A is being protected from the data point it most needs to weigh.

## Frame-level objection the orchestrator should carry forward

The "structural fix" in Option A is prose-on-prose; without a mandatory mechanical gate (pre-commit script or agent-side validator), Option A is documentation-only and the corpus says documentation-only is worse than no rule — the generator must either promote step 6 to mandatory or downgrade A's confidence to "low."

## Loop 2 challenges

### 1. The Revision-2 frame is itself a frame error

Revision 2 says: the substrate IS the question, but *the right move now is to ship something small and name the gate-substrate as a follow-up.* That is two contradictory claims welded together. If the substrate is the question, then deferring it is not a small move — it is the move, made by default, made unconsciously, made without the design exercise the panel asked for. The first hook that ships *is* the substrate-by-precedent the architecture lens warned about; calling it "smallest concrete action" does not change its precedential weight. Revision 2 is a way to feel like the panel was heard while still shipping the panel-warned shape, just labeled differently.

**Condition under which Revision 2 is wrong:** if the "smallest concrete action" the loop-2 candidate proposes is itself a `PostToolUse` hook, a `bin/` script, or any mechanism that exposes a verdict-and-bypass contract — even in warn-only mode — then Revision 2 has shipped the substrate while claiming to defer it. The deferral is rhetorical, not structural. The architecture lens's veto applies unchanged.

### 2. Steelman — Option Zero, do nothing now

The strongest case for no action: the 50/50 split is not a defect, it is *information*. It tells the operator two things — (a) the rule is not load-bearing enough for the absence of enforcement to have caused real harm (zero privacy leaks, zero broken grep workflows reported), and (b) agents and operator both produce both styles depending on context, which is the actual organic behavior of the system. Acting on a 50/50 split with a hook, a slash-command patch, or even a mass sweep imposes a discipline the system has not earned by demonstrating it needs. The cheapest correct response is: fix only the misworded reason (c) — that is unambiguous, no-tradeoff, factually-required maintenance — and let the next 90 days produce evidence. If the split moves to 70/30 organically (in either direction), the system is converging; act then. If a real harm shows up (a leak, a broken link, a confused new contributor), act on the harm. **No action is correct when the cost of the wrong action exceeds the cost of the status quo, and the status quo has produced no measurable harm.** This is the case here: the panel's three vetoes describe the cost of acting; the explore audit describes the cost of not acting (zero). Doing nothing now respects that asymmetry.

### 3. Steelman — Option Negative, delete clause 2

Delete clause 2 entirely; keep only clauses 1, 3, 4, 5 (privacy, outside-repo, consent, no-quoting). Authors and agents use whatever style they produce naturally. Grep-stability is sacrificed; everything else is preserved. The rule's footprint shrinks to its uncontested core — exactly the surface area the explore audit confirms is empirically working. This is the right move when (a) grep-stability turns out to be a stated benefit nobody actually consumes (no orchestrator pass currently greps across both styles; no measurement shows the cost of style-mixed grep is non-zero), (b) the cost of every future enforcement debate exceeds the value of the property being enforced, and (c) the operator is willing to admit that the rule's grep-stability clause was an *aspirational* property, not an *observed* one. Option Negative is the move that *retires the debate* rather than re-opening it every quarter. It is also the move most consistent with the meta-challenge: the operator violated their own rule and the rule's strongest defender (the operator) cannot articulate what concrete capability is lost by deletion. When the rule's author cannot name the cost of deletion in concrete terms, deletion is the honest move.

### 4. Distinguishing a real "smallest action" from a non-decision dressed as one

A genuinely-small first step has three properties: (i) it is reversible in under five minutes with a single edit (a prose change in one file qualifies; a hook in `.claude/settings.json` plus a `bin/` script does not — that is two files plus a behavior change in the runtime), (ii) it commits to no contract that future actions must honor (fixing the wording of reason (c) commits to nothing; shipping a warn-only hook commits to a verdict format, a log location, a bypass mechanism, and the precedent that gates are bash scripts), and (iii) the action is justified on its own terms without reference to a future larger action it is "preparing for" (if the only justification is "this lets us decide the substrate later," it is not a small step — it is a down-payment on a decision that has not been made). A non-decision dressed as a step fails all three: it has rollback cost, it sets contract precedent, and its justification is forward-looking. The loop-2 candidate must be tested against these three; failing any one means the "smallness" is rhetorical.

### 5. Honesty meta-challenge, deepened — is the workflow drifting with the operator?

The trajectory: operator authors a punch-list violating clause 2 → loop 1 recommends preserving the rule with mandatory enforcement → panel vetoes → loop 2 reframes toward "smallest concrete action." Each reframe has moved the recommendation *away* from the previous critique's specific objection, rather than toward a position the operator was committed to before the critique landed. That is the signature of *defensive reframing*, not principled reframing. A principled reframe would name a position the operator holds independently of the panel and defend it; a defensive reframe finds whatever shape the panel didn't object to and parks there. The fact that loop 1's "mandatory enforcement" became loop 2's "smallest action / defer the substrate" is a 180° flip on the load-bearing variable (enforcement strength), accomplished without the operator stating *"I changed my mind about enforcement, here is why."* The workflow is generating motion, but it may be generating motion *around* the operator's actual position rather than *toward* a defensible one. The frame-challenger's call: the loop-2 candidate must include a one-paragraph statement from the orchestrator naming what the operator believes *independent of the panel* about path discipline — and if that paragraph cannot be written without contradicting either loop 1's recommendation or the punch-list as authored, the workflow has drifted into shape-matching and the right action is to escalate to synthesis-with-disagreements rather than ship loop 2 at all.

### 6. Condition under which the workflow itself is the wrong tool

The workflow is for design questions — questions where the choice has architectural consequence and the cost of the wrong answer compounds. Path-discipline may be a *housekeeping* question: pick a convention, write it down, move on; the cost of the wrong choice is a one-time sweep, not a compounding architectural debt. Three signals that the workflow is the wrong tool here: (a) the panel's three vetoes describe failure modes (substrate-by-accident, change-management blast radius, contract-UX gap) that *only exist because the workflow is producing a candidate big enough to have those failure modes* — a 30-second prose-edit decision has none of them; (b) the orchestrator has now spent two full loops of subagent invocations on a 50/50 style split with zero observed harm, which is the definition of structure inflating a question past its stakes; (c) the loop-2 frame's own escape hatch ("ship something small") is an admission that the question does not warrant the apparatus around it. **The workflow is the wrong tool when the cost of running it exceeds the worst-case cost of getting the answer wrong by intuition.** For this question, the worst case of an intuitive call is "we picked a style, half the files are wrong, we sweep them in 20 minutes." That is below the cost of two critic-panel rounds. The honest move may be: invoke `quick take`, pick a style, fix reason (c), close the punch-list item, and treat the meta-lesson — *we ran the full workflow on a housekeeping question* — as the actual finding worth capturing in `upgrades/`.
