## Frame as given

## Alternative frame

**There is no doc problem. There is an *audience* problem.**

Every framing in [frame.md](../2026-04-27-item-04-stale-workflow-docs/frame.md) — including the two alts — treats the question as *which doc-shape is right*. But the canon-librarian's distillation surfaced the un-asked upstream question explicitly (Gap #4): *whom is this repo's workflow doc for?* Three candidate audiences are silently bundled today — (i) Claude reading [CLAUDE.md](../../../CLAUDE.md) into context, (ii) a human contributor landing on [README.md](../../../README.md), (iii) a forker / external reader who wants to understand the stack before adopting it. The *correct* doc shape is fully determined by which of those audiences the maintainer is actually serving, and the inverse: as long as the audience is unnamed, every doc decision (delete, consolidate, expand, defer) is locally defensible and globally arbitrary, which is exactly the symptom the punch-list keeps re-encountering. Under this frame the verb is **declare**, not prune: name the primary audience for the workflow surface, then let the doc shape fall out. *"Delete the stale routing"* is still right tactically, but it is a side-effect of the declaration, not the decision itself — and skipping the declaration is what produces the next stale-doc punch-list item six months out.

This is a frame, not a recommendation: it does not say *which* audience to pick, and any of the three is defensible. The point is that picking one is upstream of picking a doc shape.

## Condition under which the current frame is wrong

The current frame is wrong if a non-trivial fraction of [README.md](../../../README.md) readers in the next 6–12 months are *forkers evaluating adoption* rather than *the maintainer or Claude itself* — because that audience needs a Heilmeier-grade jargon-free *"what is this and why"* that neither [CLAUDE.md](../../../CLAUDE.md) (operator-dense) nor [.claude/session-artifacts/README.md](../../README.md) (layout-only) provides, and option (a)'s "point at two existing docs" silently ships a category-mismatched onboarding to that audience.

## Engagement with canon contradicting passages

**SRE ch.28's onboarding-artifact prescription (canon fact #6, librarian contradiction A vs. B).** Google's SRE book — the *same chapter* that warns about doc rot — also prescribes a dedicated, first-class learning checklist as a "social contract" for on-call onboarding. The current frame leans entirely on the doc-rot side of that chapter (collapse to canonical, minimum surface) and treats the prescription side as inapplicable because this stack has no team. That asymmetry is the frame's largest unexamined move. The challenge: if the audience reframe above lands on "forkers / external adopters," the SRE prescription becomes directly relevant — adopters *are* the on-call newbies in the analogy, and the prescription says give them a dedicated artifact, not a pointer to two docs. The current frame survives only if the audience is *the maintainer plus Claude* and forkers are out of scope.

**Heilmeier on jargon-free explanation (canon fact #7).** The canon librarian inferred (inference #4) that option (c) is the strongest play *by Heilmeier's standard* because it co-locates explanation with artifacts. The current frame ([frame.md](../2026-04-27-item-04-stale-workflow-docs/frame.md) §"Does the reframe change the answer?") rejects (b) and treats (c) as nice-to-have, but does not engage Heilmeier head-on: neither [CLAUDE.md](../../../CLAUDE.md) nor [.claude/session-artifacts/README.md](../../README.md) answers *"what are you trying to do, in absolutely no jargon, and who cares?"* If a new reader cannot derive that from the post-(a) reading order, the frame's claim that *"option (a) closes the correctness gap"* is technically true (no false claims remain) but functionally hollow (no true claims about purpose are added). The challenge is not *"write more docs"* — it is that the frame's definition of "correctness" is *absence of falsehood*, and Heilmeier's standard is *presence of purpose-in-plain-language*. Those are not the same bar.

**Anthropic's exploration-cost claim (canon fact #8, librarian contradiction C vs. D).** The frame leans on Anthropic fact #3 (JIT retrieval bypasses stale indexing) to defend [CLAUDE.md](../../../CLAUDE.md) as "the surface that has to stay current anyway." Fact #8 from the same essay is the counter-weight: JIT retrieval imposes exploration cost on every reader who lacks up-front guidance, and "without proper guidance, an agent can waste context by misusing tools, chasing dead-ends, or failing to identify key information." The current frame asserts the exploration cost is acceptable for this stack's onboarding population without *naming* that population (this is the same gap as the audience reframe above). The challenge: option (a)'s defense requires showing that *for the stated audience*, the exploration cost from [README.md](../../../README.md) → [CLAUDE.md](../../../CLAUDE.md) is genuinely low. If the audience is Claude, the cost is ~zero (CLAUDE.md is auto-loaded). If the audience is a forker, the cost is high (CLAUDE.md is operator-dense and assumes the 12-step framing already). The frame currently asserts *low* without naming *for whom*.

## Challenge to the classifier label

The classifier's primary label is **replace** with **refactor** as the alternative, and **investigation** as secondary on the meta-sequencing question. The substantive alternative worth surfacing is none of those three: it is **new** — specifically, "we should treat this as the first iteration of building a workflow doc, not the last iteration of cleaning one up." Under that classification, the question is not *"what do we do about the stale file?"* (which yields *delete and redirect*) but *"what is the minimum new artifact that addresses the audience the redirect targets do not already serve?"* (which yields *write a one-page Heilmeier-style README front-matter or a `whatis.md`*). The downstream frame change is significant: under **replace**, the unit of work is the stale file and the success criterion is its removal; under **new**, the unit of work is the audience-shaped gap and the success criterion is whether a stated reader can answer Heilmeier's first two questions in one read. The classifier was not wrong to label this **replace** — the user framed it that way and the immediate work *is* a deletion — but the **new** alternative is substantively different, not just a relabel, and the orchestrator should carry it.

## Frame-level objection the orchestrator should carry forward

The frame names the doc but not the reader; until the primary audience for the workflow surface is declared, every option (a/b/c, defer/bundle) is locally defensible and the next stale-doc punch-list item is already accruing.
