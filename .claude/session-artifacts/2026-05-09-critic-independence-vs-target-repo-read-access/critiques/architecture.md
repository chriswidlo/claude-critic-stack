# Critic — Architecture lens

## 1. Weakest structural link

The candidate places the load-bearing **inter-input independence** invariant on **orchestrator discipline at candidate.md authorship time** (move 3) — a procedural rule embedded in CLAUDE.md prose, with no enforcement primitive, no schema, and no observable artifact that distinguishes a "target-paraphrasing" candidate from a "quote-only" candidate. The contract has three parties (orchestrator, lens, comparator) but only one of them can verify compliance, and that one (the orchestrator) is also the only one that can violate it. This is the architectural shape of a self-checking author. Every long-running discipline rule in this stack that lacked a checkable artifact has degraded — see the **`critique-start` line 38–42 "if they verify facts"** clause that the candidate itself is removing because it became ambiguous under exactly the same pressure. Move 3 reproduces that defect at a higher layer of the stack.

The fix is not "stronger wording." It is a typed candidate (one per lens, each composed against that lens's allowed input set) or a separate `candidate-invariants.md` artifact whose schema forbids target-repo prose by construction. The candidate considers the typed-candidate alternative only inside assumption A2's failure branch, never as a primary option.

## 2. Invariants at risk (the candidate does not name these)

1. **Decision-log monotonicity.** Move 2 promotes `decision-log.md` from a session-narration artifact (currently used only for replan-vs-rewrite at step 11) to a *permission grant ledger* that gates pass-through of `$CRITIC_TARGET` to a named lens. This silently changes its type from append-only narrative to a security-relevant capability table. Nothing in CLAUDE.md or in the skills enforces "a `$CRITIC_TARGET` grant must precede the step-10 invocation it authorizes." A grant written *after* the lens has run is indistinguishable on disk from one written before. Break: the audit trail claim is unverifiable; the contract becomes prose-only.

2. **Single-direction dependency between session-state and skill-state.** `critique-start` writes a session-scoped variable (`$CRITIC_TARGET`); the orchestrator at step 10 reads `decision-log.md` to decide whether to pass that variable. The candidate's move 2 introduces a back-edge from a session artifact (`decision-log.md`) into a skill's runtime contract. Today skills depend on session state, not the reverse. Break: skill behavior becomes a function of arbitrary writes to a markdown file that no skill currently parses, owns, or schema-validates.

3. **Lens isomorphism (the same prompt receives the same input class across lenses).** Today, all three lenses receive the same input set. Move 2 makes per-lens input membership a configurable axis — lens A may have `$CRITIC_TARGET`, lens B may not. The minority-veto rule presumes lenses produce verdicts on the *same object*. Once inputs diverge by lens, "the same object" is no longer architecturally guaranteed; it is asserted by orchestrator discipline. Break: the comparator's `agree | partial-agree | disagree` classes become uninterpretable when one lens read `<target>/...` and the other did not — the comparator is comparing apples to grounded apples and reporting "agree" when both happen to land on `rework` for unrelated reasons.

4. **`<target>/...` rewrite as a privacy primitive only.** The path-discipline regime is explicitly silent on information content (Explore §17–18; challenges.md §"Path-discipline"). Move 3's "quote target-repo content via `<target>/...` references" overloads the rewrite token with a *second* contract: it now also marks "this content was deliberately admitted to candidate.md as a quotation." Same syntax, two semantics, no separator. Break: future readers (and future tools) cannot distinguish "this is a path that got rewritten" from "this is a deliberate quotation gate."

5. **Idempotence of the experiment in move 4.** Running one shadow-panel session with one shadow receiving paraphrased candidate.md and the other receiving original is a two-cell A/B with N=1. The candidate calls this "the cheap experiment" without naming the architectural property it produces (a measurement) versus what it actually produces (an anecdote). Break: a single-session result will be used to retire or adopt a primitive whose blast radius is the entire panel — outside-view base-rate territory, not within-tolerance evidence.

## 3. Coupling and direction

- **Coupling reversal: CLAUDE.md → comparator.** Move 1 rewrites CLAUDE.md's panel claim to "decorrelated by role/aspect on a shared input … When `SHADOW_PANEL=1`, the comparator adds inter-model decorrelation on identical inputs." This makes the orchestrator-facing public claim **dependent on whether the shadow panel ran**. The volatile thing (an env var, set per-session) now appears in the stable thing (the panel's stated identity in CLAUDE.md). Dependency points from stable to volatile — a layering violation. The right shape is the inverse: CLAUDE.md states the panel's *unconditional* property; `SHADOW_PANEL=1` documentation states what shadow adds *on top*. Today CLAUDE.md is closer to that shape than the candidate's revision is.

- **Coupling introduced: `decision-log.md` ↔ step 10 invocation.** Today `decision-log.md` is read at step 11 (replan-vs-rewrite). Move 2 makes it a step-10 *input gate*. Step 11 depends on step 10's output; if step 10 now also depends on a file that is read at step 11, you have at minimum a temporal ordering constraint that is not encoded anywhere, and at worst a cycle once a future skill reasonably treats `decision-log.md` as the source of truth for "what was passed to whom."

- **Implicit dependency: comparator on candidate-composition discipline.** Move 4's experiment design uses the comparator to measure inter-input correlation. The comparator was designed to compare verdicts on identical inputs (Explore §23, §inferred 5). Feeding it differing inputs (paraphrased vs. original) and reading its `agree | partial-agree | disagree` output as a measurement of inter-input correlation **inverts the comparator's semantics**: agreement now means "candidate paraphrasing did not change the verdict," not "the lenses agree on the candidate." The comparator's contract changes silently, with no version, no flag, no separate artifact. This is a protocol-surface violation.

## 4. Ignored architectural alternatives

1. **Typed candidate (per-lens candidate.md).** Compose three candidate artifacts at step 9 — one per lens — each authored against that lens's allowed input set. The orchestrator's discipline becomes "do not include target-repo prose in the candidate for any lens that has not been granted target access." This is checkable (the artifacts are on disk and diffable) rather than aspirational (move 3's note in CLAUDE.md). The candidate considers this only inside A2's failure branch.

2. **Invariants-only feed to a designated narrow lens.** Per outside-view §9 and scope-map's "operations as the only plausible isolation candidate," route a labeled `target-invariants.md` (produced by Explore, schema-constrained: numeric/boolean invariants only, no narrative) to one lens. Other lenses receive nothing target-shaped. This separates the *information content* concern from the *path rendering* concern that `<target>/...` was built for, which the candidate's move 3 conflates.

3. **Frame-level alternative the candidate is one move away from but does not take: collapse the panel to a single rubric judge with shadow triangulation** (canon §9 directly supports this). The candidate cites canon §9 in A3's failure branch as a *fallback* but does not put it on the table as a primary architectural alternative, despite move 1 already conceding the panel's independence claim shrinks to "role-orthogonality on shared input + optional cross-model triangulation" — which is most of the way to "single judge, two prompts (or two models)" already.

## 5. Frame-level objection

**The candidate's recommendation is shaped like a contract change but is functionally a claim change.** Moves 1, 2, and 3 mostly rewrite *what the stack says about itself* (CLAUDE.md prose, a SKILL.md clause, a discipline note). Move 4 defers the only thing that would distinguish a real architectural change from a documentation change. The frame the candidate inherits ("decide a stance + diagnose depth") is a *positioning* frame — what claim does the panel make. Architecture cannot adjudicate positioning. If the question is "what should we tell readers about the panel," the right artifact is a README revision, not a panel modification, and the architecture lens has nothing to add. If the question is "what should the panel actually be," then move 1 is downstream of moves 2–4, not upstream — you do not get to restate a claim before you have changed the thing the claim is about.

The frame the candidate should be operating under is: *the panel's current architecture (three Opus lenses, shared input, minority-veto) does not enforce any independence property below role-orthogonality; either (a) make role-orthogonality the only claim and remove minority-veto (because veto across non-commensurable verdicts is theatrical, per challenges.md), or (b) introduce an enforcement primitive (typed candidate, invariants-only feed, or single-judge-with-comparator) that makes a stronger claim true.* The candidate picks neither — it shrinks the claim (option a's first half) without removing the veto (option a's second half) and without introducing the enforcement primitive (option b). The result is the architecturally weakest of the three positions: a smaller claim, the same mechanism, and a discipline note that no artifact can verify.

## 6. Verdict

**rework.**

What would change the verdict to `approve`: replace move 3 (discipline note in CLAUDE.md) with a **typed-candidate primitive** (one candidate.md per lens at step 9, composed against that lens's allowed input set), and replace move 4's single-session A/B with a stated measurement contract (what input to the comparator, what output class means what, written into `critic-comparator.md`'s protocol surface) before the experiment runs.
