## What was observed

The operator asked for a folder where profound upgrade ideas could land. The 12-step workflow ran end-to-end, produced two candidate lab designs (after one replan loop), and arrived at a synthesis with eight named dimensions, four bash scripts, three operational controls, and a multi-month time-box. Architecture and operations critics approved on loop 2. The operator read the synthesis and called it process-heavy and content-light.

The corrective design (this very lab) used roughly 5% of what the workflow produced and added what the workflow missed (visible meta tables, state spine, free-form body).

## Why this is structural, not embarrassing

Each individual workflow step does its job correctly when it adds structure. Classifier surfaces a frame; frame-challenger names alternatives; scope-mapper enumerates primitives; generator produces a candidate addressing all named concerns; panel critiques what could go wrong. The cumulative effect of every step doing its job correctly is a candidate that is more designed than the question warrants.

There is no step in the workflow whose job is to ask: *is the answer to this question smaller than what we are about to produce?* The frame-challenger comes closest but it challenges the *frame*, not the *output magnitude*. The panel critiques what's missing or wrong, not what's superfluous. Synthesis presents the post-critique recommendation, not the question of whether it should be 10% of itself.

This is correlated bias toward elaboration across the entire stack — not a bug in any single agent.

## What an under-design counter-bias might look like

Three candidates, increasing in invasiveness:

- A **minimalist lens** added to the panel whose only job is asking "could this candidate be 1/4 of itself and still solve the problem?" — same minority-veto authority.
- A **frame-challenger discipline** that always names the smallest-possible-answer as one of the alternative frames.
- A **pre-synthesis check**: before presenting the recommendation, the orchestrator must answer "if the operator wanted the smallest answer that could work, what would I tell them?" and surface that alongside the full recommendation.

Each is small. The bias being mitigated is large because it is structural — every workflow run carries it.

## Open questions

- Is this bias proportional to the question's "design surface" (number of named dimensions in the input), or does it fire equally on simple questions?
- Would a minimalist lens just produce minimalist verdicts that get over-ruled by other lenses? If so, the bias is deeper than any single lens can correct.
- Could this entry's own existence (writing the failure down) make the next workflow run notice and self-correct? Or does in-context warning fail to override structural bias?
